    Rheumatic Fever

    Post-streptococcal inflammatory disease • Jones criteria • secondary prophylaxis
    Quick revision: A nonsuppurative inflammatory complication of group A streptococcal infection affecting the heart, joints, CNS, skin, and subcutaneous tissue. Diagnosis uses Jones criteria with evidence of antecedent GAS infection.
    Definition & Predisposing Factors

    Definition: A nonsuppurative acute inflammatory complication of group A streptococcal infection involving principally the heart, joints, central nervous system, skin, and subcutaneous tissue.

    Predisposing factors

    • Age: Most cases occur between 5–15 years.
    • Sex: No overall sex predilection, except mitral valve prolapse and chorea occur more often in females.
    • Familial susceptibility: Significant.
    • Season: More common in colder and wetter months.
    • Infection history: Recurrent streptococcal infections.
    • Socioeconomic factors: Malnutrition, overcrowding, and lower socioeconomic status.
    Pathology

    Cardiac involvement

    Pancarditis involving the pericardium, myocardium, and endocardium, with fibrinoid degeneration, cellular infiltration, and Aschoff nodules affecting all three layers.

    Extracardiac involvement

    • Joints: Nonspecific edema and hyperemia of inflamed synovial membranes.
    • Brain: Meningo-encephalitis.
    • Skin: Subcutaneous nodules consisting of collections of Aschoff bodies.
    • Lungs / pleura: Pleurisy and pneumonia.
    • Valves: Valvulitis.
    Jones Diagnostic Criteria
    Important examination point

    Diagnosis requires two major criteria or one major plus two minor criteria, together with evidence of antecedent group A streptococcal infection.

    Major criteria

    • Carditis — endocarditis, myocarditis, or pericarditis.
    • Polyarthritis — large joints, hot/red/swollen, fleeting or migratory, dramatic salicylate response; more marked in adults.
    • Sydenham chorea — involuntary, spontaneous, semipurposive, non-repetitive movements at rest; increase with emotions and disappear during sleep; associated hypotonia, emotional lability, and pendular knee jerk; may follow a long latent period of several months.
    • Erythema marginatum — rare; trunk and proximal extremities; spreads peripherally with central clearing.
    • Subcutaneous nodules — rare; small, painless, non-tender swellings over bony prominences, subcutaneous and attached to tendon sheaths.

    Minor criteria

    • Arthralgia.
    • Fever.
    • Elevated ESR.
    • Elevated CRP.
    • Prolonged PR interval on ECG.

    Carditis details

    • Mitral valvulitis: Apical mid-diastolic Carey Coombs murmur from swollen, stiff mitral cusps. An apical systolic murmur radiating to the axilla with muffled S1 and S3 indicates permanent mitral regurgitation.
    • Aortic valvulitis: Early diastolic murmur over the base and left sternal border; a systolic murmur may also be heard over the aortic area.
    • Myocarditis: Tachycardia disproportionate to fever, often muffled S1, gallop rhythm, rapid cardiac enlargement, and heart failure.
    • Pericarditis: Dry or accompanied by effusion.
    Investigations
    • ECG: Prolonged PR interval, various degrees of heart block, and ST–T changes.
    • Blood picture: Anemia and leukocytosis may occur.
    • ASO titre: Elevated antistreptolysin O titre.
    • Acute-phase reactants: Elevated ESR and CRP.
    Treatment & Secondary Prophylaxis

    Curative treatment

    • Bed rest.
    • Light, low-salt diet.
    • Benzathine penicillin: 1.2 million units IM.
    • Aspirin: 100–125 mg/kg/day in children; 6–8 g/day in adults, divided into 4–5 doses until satisfactory response.
    • NSAIDs.
    • Prednisone: 60–120 mg/day in 4 divided doses until response; reduce to 25 mg/day and continue for 4–6 weeks, then taper gradually to prevent post-steroidal rebound. Start salicylates while steroids are tapered and continue for 2–3 additional weeks. Indicated with myocarditis and pericarditis.
    • Chorea: Complete mental and physical rest; diazepam or chlorpromazine.
    • Heart failure: Antifailure measures such as digoxin and diuretics.

    Antistreptococcal prophylaxis

    • Continue after an acute attack to prevent recurrence.
    • Preferred regimen: Benzathine penicillin G 1.2 million units IM monthly.
    • Duration depends on age, occupation, household circumstances, cardiac status, and time since the previous attack.
    • If cardiac involvement is present, continue prophylaxis until 45 years of age.

    Mitral Stenosis Valve obstruction

    Rheumatic anatomy • LA pressure overload • opening snap and diastolic rumble
    Quick revision: Usually chronic rheumatic disease. Symptoms become important below 2.0 cm²; severe MS is 1.0 cm² or less. Classic auscultation: loud S1, opening snap, and a low-pitched diastolic rumble with presystolic accentuation.
    Etiology & Anatomic Features

    Etiology

    • Almost always chronic rheumatic heart disease after one or more attacks of acute rheumatic fever.
    • Rare: congenital MS, infective endocarditis with large obstructing vegetations, and massive mitral annular calcification.

    Rheumatic anatomy

    • Diffuse fibrous thickening of closure margins.
    • Fibrous thickening of anterior and posterior leaflets with rigidity.
    • Fusion of one or both commissures.
    • Shortened, thickened, fused chordae causing subvalvular stenosis.
    • Calcific deposits in one or both leaflets.
    • Aschoff nodules in the myocardium.
    • Abnormal transvalvular flow patterns produced by acute rheumatic fever.
    Pathophysiology & Hemodynamics
    • Normal area: 4–6 cm².
    • A clinically important LA–LV gradient develops when the area falls below 2.0 cm².
    • Exercise markedly increases LA pressure, causing pulmonary congestion and exertional dyspnea.
    • Severe MS: Area ≤1.0 cm² with elevated resting LA pressure and an approximate 20 mmHg transvalvular gradient.
    • LV volume is normal or decreased.

    Pulmonary hypertension stages

    1. Passive pulmonary hypertension.
    2. Reactive pulmonary hypertension from pulmonary arteriolar vasoconstriction.
    3. Permanent pulmonary hypertension → RV dilatation/failure, often with secondary tricuspid regurgitation from annular dilatation.
    Clinical Picture & Complications
    • Raised LA/pulmonary venous/capillary pressure → dyspnea, pulmonary edema, and hemoptysis.
    • Dilated LA → atrial fibrillation and systemic embolism.
    • Pulmonary hypertension → RV failure with fatigue, peripheral edema, ascites, cyanosis, and venous thromboembolism.
    • Roughened abnormal valve surface → infective endocarditis.
    Physical Signs & Murmur

    Inspection and palpation

    • Malar flush.
    • Peripheral cyanosis in severe MS.
    • JVD with RV failure.
    • Palpitation.
    • Parasternal RV impulse.
    • Palpable pulmonary arterial impulse.
    • Palpable S1, P2, and sometimes the diastolic rumble.
    S1Increased intensity.
    Additional soundOpening snap.
    MurmurLow-pitched diastolic rumble with presystolic accentuation.
    Investigations

    ECG

    • Atrial fibrillation is common.
    • P mitrale in sinus rhythm: prolonged M-shaped P wave and marked terminal negative component in V1.
    • RVH in severe MS.

    Chest X-ray

    • LA enlargement.
    • Upper-lobe redistribution of pulmonary vascular flow.
    • Mitral valve calcification.
    • Enlarged pulmonary artery and RV.
    • Kerley A/B lines and interstitial pulmonary edema.

    Echocardiography

    2D echo and Doppler accurately define severity and extent of the pathological process.

    Management

    Medical

    • Diuretics for pulmonary congestion, exertional dyspnea, PND, and pulmonary edema.
    • Anticoagulation to prevent cerebral or peripheral arterial embolism.
    • Prophylactic antibiotics against infective endocarditis and treatment if present.
    • Digoxin, β-blockers, or antiarrhythmic agents for atrial fibrillation.

    Percutaneous balloon valvotomy

    • Symptomatic MS, including after previous surgical or catheter valvuloplasty.
    • Pulmonary hypertension is not a contraindication.
    • History of arterial embolism requires careful review; the extracted wording was internally inconsistent and should be interpreted with the original source context.
    • More than mild MR or clinically significant AS → refer for surgery.

    Surgery

    • Surgical commissurotomy.
    • MVR for double mitral lesion, calcific MS, or history of embolism / LAA thrombus confirmed by TEE.

    Mitral Regurgitation Volume overload

    LV + LA volume overload • pansystolic murmur radiating to axilla
    Quick revision: Chronic MR causes LA and LV volume overload with eccentric hypertrophy. A mildly reduced LVEF is serious because EF may remain apparently normal despite declining contractility.
    Etiology

    Rheumatic MR produces progressive fibrosis, contracture, and calcification of valve and chordae, causing predominant MS, predominant MR, or mixed disease.

    Chronic MR

    • Inflammatory: Rheumatic heart disease, SLE, progressive systemic sclerosis.
    • Degenerative: Myxomatous degeneration, mitral annular calcification, Marfan syndrome, Ehlers–Danlos syndrome.
    • Infectious: Infective endocarditis.
    • Structural: Ruptured chordae progressing from acute to chronic; annular dilatation secondary to LV dilatation/cardiomyopathy.
    • Congenital.

    Acute MR

    • Papillary muscle dysfunction or rupture in ischemic heart disease.
    • Prosthetic valve malfunction.
    • Infective endocarditis.
    Pathophysiology & Hemodynamics
    • Chronic MR imposes volume overload on both LV and LA.
    • Part of each systolic stroke volume ejects retrogradely into the LA, causing abnormal LA expansion and dilatation.
    • Eccentric LV hypertrophy develops; contractile function may be preserved for years.
    • Long-standing dilatation causes interstitial LV fibrosis and declining contractility.
    • LVEF caveat: EF may stay normal or near-normal despite impaired contractility; mildly reduced LV function is a serious sign.
    • Raised pulmonary capillary wedge pressure may cause pulmonary arterial hypertension and RV failure, generally later/slower than in MS because a compliant dilated LA buffers pressure.
    Symptoms
    • Exertional dyspnea, progressing to orthopnea, PND, rest dyspnea, and pulmonary edema.
    • Fatigue and generalized weakness from reduced cardiac output.
    • Peripheral edema and ascites late in disease due to RV failure from pulmonary hypertension.
    Physical Examination & Murmur
    • Anxious facies and diaphoresis with LV failure.
    • JVP distention with RV failure.
    • Small or normal carotid volume.
    • Rales; basal dullness and diminished breath sounds.
    • Laterally displaced, enlarged apical impulse.
    • Reduced S1.
    • S3 may be present.
    • Hepatomegaly, ascites, and peripheral edema with RV failure.
    TimingHolosystolic / pansystolic.
    MaximumApex.
    RadiationAxilla.
    Investigations
    • ECG: Nonspecific; may show LVH, LA enlargement, and AF.
    • Chest X-ray: LV and LA enlargement; rarely evident in acute MR.
    • Echocardiography: Diagnoses MR, defines cause/severity, assesses LV function, LA dimensions, pulmonary artery pressure, and associated stenosis.
    • Cardiac catheterization: Helps define etiology, particularly acute MR associated with AMI, IHD, or LV dilatation.
    Management
    • Medical treatment parallels MS: symptom control, diuretics, and management of heart failure and arrhythmias.
    • Surgery in severe disease.
    • Valve replacement is described as the surgical procedure of choice.
    • Mitral valve repair in selected cases.
    • Correct the cause when applicable, such as LA myxoma removal or CABG for acute MR secondary to AMI.

    Mitral Valve Prolapse

    Floppy valve • Barlow syndrome • click–murmur syndrome
    Overview, Anatomy & Genetics
    • Also called floppy valve syndrome, Barlow syndrome, click–murmur syndrome, or myxomatous mitral valve.
    • Affects 3–5% of the population.
    • Exaggerated posterior bowing of one or both mitral leaflets into the LA during systole.
    • May occur with or without MR.
    • Redundant myxomatous tissue with excess proteoglycan deposition in the spongiosa (middle layer) enlarges the valve and causes systolic prolapse.
    • Strong genetic transmission; sometimes autosomal dominant.
    Diagnosis

    Auscultation

    • Mid-to-late systolic click with late systolic murmur.
    • Apical late systolic or holosystolic murmur in young patients.

    2D echocardiography

    • Mild-to-moderate or marked superior systolic displacement of mitral leaflets.
    • Mild-to-moderate MR.
    Management
    • Usually benign; reassurance is sufficient.
    • Antibiotic prophylaxis against infective endocarditis for dental or surgical procedures, as stated in the source.
    • Symptomatic treatment of arrhythmias; β-blockers may be used for palpitations as preserved from the base guide.
    • Surgical repair or replacement if severe MR develops.

    Aortic Stenosis Fixed outflow obstruction

    Pressure overload • LVH • angina, syncope, dyspnea
    Quick revision: Critical AS is a peak systolic gradient >50 mmHg with normal output or valve area <0.8 cm². Pulse is parvus et tardus; murmur is harsh ejection systolic at the 2nd right intercostal space radiating to the carotids.
    Etiology & Classification

    Valvular

    • Congenital bicuspid valve: Most common cause; severe disease generally in the fifth decade or later.
    • Rheumatic: Almost always accompanied by rheumatic mitral involvement.
    • Calcification: Idiopathic or secondary to congenital/rheumatic disease.

    Subvalvular

    • Dynamic: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with marked LV, especially septal, hypertrophy.
    • Fixed: Membranous diaphragm or fibrous ridge just below the valve.

    Supravalvular

    Membranous, hourglass, or hypoplastic narrowing of the ascending aorta immediately above the valve.

    Pathophysiology
    • Reduced LV stroke output → reduced peripheral and coronary blood flow.
    • LV pressure overload → concentric LVH and later LV failure.
    • Critical obstruction: Peak gradient >50 mmHg with normal cardiac output, or valve area <0.8 cm² (less than one-third normal).
    • Resting cardiac output is often normal early, but fails to rise during exercise; later both output and transvalvular gradient decline.
    • Coronary perfusion may be impaired even without CAD because hypertrophied myocardium compresses penetrating coronary vessels.
    Clinical Picture & Prognosis

    Mild disease may be asymptomatic. Severe disease produces the classic triad:

    1. Exertional syncope: Exercise vasodilation with fixed cardiac output or sudden arrhythmia.
    2. Angina: LVH plus reduced coronary flow.
    3. Exertional dyspnea / CHF: LV failure.

    Physical Signs & Murmur
    • Pulse: Slowly rising, small volume, delayed peak — pulsus parvus et tardus.
    • BP: Low systolic pressure and narrow pulse pressure in severe disease.
    • Systolic thrill over carotids and over the 2nd right intercostal space.
    • Signs of LVH.
    • Systolic ejection click from rigid cusps; disappears when calcified.
    • Weak, delayed A2.
    • S4 at the apex in severe disease.
    TimingSystolic ejection.
    Character / siteHarsh, rasping; 2nd right ICS at parasternal edge.
    RadiationUpward to neck arteries; sometimes toward apex.
    Complications
    • Sudden death from ventricular fibrillation, cardiac standstill, or myocardial infarction.
    • Infective endocarditis.
    • Left ventricular failure.
    Investigations
    • ECG: LVH with strain in most severe cases.
    • Chest X-ray: LVH, post-stenotic ascending aortic dilatation, and valve calcification.
    • Echo/Doppler: LVH, bright thick echoes in the aortic root, and transvalvular gradient estimation.
    • Cardiac catheterization: Precise systolic gradient across the valve.
    Management
    • Medical strategy is described as similar to MS for symptom relief and monitoring.
    • AVR: Indicated in symptomatic adults with valve area <0.8 cm².
    • Balloon valvoplasty: Children/young adults with congenital AS or bridge/palliation for patients too ill for open surgery.

    Chronic Aortic Regurgitation Wide pulse pressure

    LV volume overload • collapsing pulse • early diastolic decrescendo murmur
    Quick revision: Chronic AR causes high stroke volume, wide pulse pressure, LV dilatation, and reduced diastolic coronary perfusion. Listen sitting forward in forced expiration.
    Etiology
    • Rheumatic fever — stated as the commonest cause.
    • Infective endocarditis.
    • Congenital bicuspid aortic valve.
    • Ascending aortic dilatation from syphilitic aortitis, ankylosing spondylitis, cystic medial necrosis, or aortic aneurysm.
    • Marfan syndrome was present in the original base guide and is retained with aortic-root disease.
    Pathophysiology
    • Aortic-to-LV diastolic regurgitation increases LV stroke volume.
    • LV volume overload → LV dilatation → LV failure; massive dilatation may produce cor bovinum.
    • Myocardial ischemia results from LV dilatation and low diastolic pressure impairing coronary perfusion.
    Symptoms
    • Generalized body throbbing from increased arterial pulsations.
    • Palpitations from forceful LV contraction.
    • Pulmonary congestion when LV failure develops.
    • Classical effort angina: Reduced coronary perfusion from low diastolic pressure.
    • Angina of Lewis: Nocturnal, treatment-resistant attacks with tachycardia, hypertension, and sweating.
    Peripheral & Cardiac Signs

    Peripheral signs

    • Collapsing / water-hammer pulse and wide pulse pressure.
    • Visible carotid pulsation.
    • Capillary pulsation with light pressure on the nail tip.
    • De Musset sign — head nodding with each beat.
    • Pistol-shot sounds over large arteries.
    • Duroziez sign — systolic and diastolic femoral murmurs under slight stethoscope compression.
    • Hill sign — popliteal systolic pressure > brachial by more than 60 mmHg.

    Cardiac auscultation

    • Signs of LV dilatation.
    • A2 diminished or absent in severe AR.
    • Early diastolic, soft decrescendo murmur, best with diaphragm while sitting up, leaning forward, and holding breath in forced expiration.
    • Maximum at left sternal border in primary valvular disease or right sternal border in aortic-root dilatation; radiates toward apex.
    • Systolic ejection flow murmur from high stroke volume.
    • S1 may be faint.
    • Austin Flint murmur: mid-diastolic and/or presystolic apical rumble caused by the AR jet fluttering the anterior mitral leaflet.
    Complications
    • Rheumatic activity.
    • Infective endocarditis.
    • Left ventricular failure.
    Investigations
    • ECG: LV dilatation and hypertrophy.
    • Chest X-ray: LV dilatation and unfolding/aneurysmal dilatation of the aorta.
    • Echocardiography: Directly visualizes and quantifies the regurgitant jet.
    Management & Follow-Up
    • Medical management parallels heart failure/mitral valve disease; afterload reduction was preserved from the base guide.
    • AVR: Significant AR when symptoms occur during ordinary activity.
    • Asymptomatic patients: close serial echocardiographic follow-up; operate when LV dysfunction begins.

    Acute Aortic Regurgitation Surgical emergency

    No time for LV adaptation • pulmonary edema • short diastolic murmur
    Etiology, Presentation & Emergency Management

    Etiology

    • Infective endocarditis.
    • Aortic dissection.
    • Trauma.

    Pathophysiology and presentation

    • The LV has no time to dilate; stroke volume falls sharply and LV diastolic pressure rises rapidly.
    • Marked pulmonary congestion and low-output symptoms occur acutely.
    • Pulse pressure is not markedly widened.
    • Classic peripheral signs of chronic AR are absent.
    • Rapid LV diastolic pressure rise prematurely closes the mitral valve, causing weak or absent S1.
    • The aortic diastolic murmur is short.
    Management: Severe acute AR is a surgical emergency requiring prompt operation.

    Systemic Hypertension

    Measurement • secondary causes • resistant disease • crises • treatment
    Quick revision: Hypertension directly contributes to MI, stroke, CHF, peripheral arterial insufficiency, and premature mortality. Accurate measurement and evaluation for target-organ damage and secondary causes are central.
    Overview, Prevention & White-Coat Hypertension

    Hypertension is a major cardiovascular risk factor directly contributing to myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular accidents, congestive heart failure, peripheral arterial insufficiency, and premature mortality.

    Its development may be slowed or prevented by preventing obesity, moderately reducing sodium intake, increasing physical activity, and avoiding excessive alcohol intake.

    White-coat hypertension

    • Persistently elevated office BP with persistently normal out-of-office BP.
    • Occurs in 20–30% of patients.
    • Most have no target-organ damage or metabolic abnormalities such as dyslipidemia or hyperinsulinemia.
    • Out-of-office readings identify white-coat hypertension and early-morning BP elevations, a period associated in the source with a high proportion of sudden deaths, MIs, and strokes.

    Guidelines for Measuring Blood Pressure

    Patient conditions

    • Sit quietly 5 minutes, back supported, arm at heart level.
    • Sitting readings usually suffice for follow-up.
    • For patients >65 years, diabetics, or those on therapy, measure immediately and 2 minutes after standing.

    Equipment

    • Cuff bladder should encircle the arm and cover two-thirds of its length; a small cuff causes spuriously high values.
    • Calibrate aneroid gauges every 6 months against a mercury manometer.
    • Use ultrasound/Doppler methods in infants.

    Technique

    • At least two readings; if they differ by >5 mmHg, repeat until two are close.
    • At least 3 sets, at least 1 week apart, to establish diagnosis.
    • Initially measure both arms; subsequently use the higher-pressure arm.
    • If arm pressure is elevated, measure one leg, especially below age 30.
    • Inflate rapidly to 20 mmHg above systolic pressure estimated by radial pulse disappearance; deflate at 3 mmHg/second.
    • Use Korotkoff phase V except in young children, where phase IV is preferred.
    • If sounds are weak, raise the arm and open/close the hand 5–10 times before inflation.
    • Record BP, posture, arm, and cuff size, e.g., 140/90, seated, right arm, large adult cuff.
    Etiology & Secondary Causes

    Primary / essential

    Accounts for the vast majority of cases; the base guide specified 90–95%.

    Secondary hypertension

    • Renal parenchymal: Acute glomerulonephritis, chronic nephritis, polycystic disease, diabetic nephropathy, hydronephrosis.
    • Renovascular: Renal artery stenosis, intrarenal vasculitis.
    • Endocrine: Acromegaly, hypo-/hyperthyroidism, hypercalcemia/hyperparathyroidism, Cushing syndrome, primary aldosteronism, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, pheochromocytoma, extra-adrenal chromaffin tumors, carcinoid.
    • Exogenous hormones: Estrogens, glucocorticoids, mineralocorticoids, sympathomimetics.
    • Coarctation of aorta.
    • Pregnancy-induced hypertension.
    • Neurologic: Raised intracranial pressure from tumor or encephalitis, respiratory acidosis.
    • Sleep apnea.
    • Acute stress: Surgery, psychogenic hyperventilation, burns, alcohol withdrawal, post-resuscitation, postoperative state.
    • Increased intravascular volume.
    • Alcohol and drug use.
    Pathogenesis of Primary Hypertension
    • Genetic predisposition: Estimated 30–60%, modified by environment.
    • Renal sodium retention: Impaired excretion expands volume and cardiac output.
    • Vascular hypertrophy: Sodium/volume retention drives contraction, remodeling, and hypertrophy, increasing peripheral resistance.
    • Sympathetic hyperactivity: Increases output and vascular tone.
    • RAAS: Renin → angiotensin I → ACE → angiotensin II, causing vasoconstriction and aldosterone-mediated sodium retention.
    • Hyperinsulinemia / insulin resistance: Associated in obese and non-obese patients; promotes atherosclerosis and alters vascular tone.
    • Endothelial dysfunction: Impaired nitric oxide synthesis and disturbed balance of local relaxing/constricting factors.
    Clinical Evaluation

    History

    • Age, sex, race, occupation, education, psychosocial issues.
    • Symptoms of TIA/CVA, angina, CHF, PVD, and claudication.
    • Pheochromocytoma: Episodic headache, palpitations, sweating, weight loss.
    • Hyperaldosteronism: Weakness, cramps, paresthesia, polyuria.
    • Cushing syndrome: Weight gain, acne, bruising, hirsutism.
    • Coarctation: Lower-limb claudication.
    • Risk factors: dyslipidemia, diabetes, smoking, high salt/saturated fat, stress, inactivity, alcohol, gout, family history of HTN/CAD/CVA/DM/dyslipidemia.
    • Comorbid renal/hepatic dysfunction, COPD/asthma, obesity, gout.
    • Pressors and fluid retainers: amphetamines, cocaine, appetite suppressants, nasal sprays, oral contraceptives, steroids, NSAIDs, cyclosporine, MAO inhibitors, erythropoietin, licorice, thyroid hormones.

    Physical examination

    • Average two BP readings, sitting/supine and standing; both arms; ankle-brachial index.
    • Height, weight, BMI.
    • Fundoscopy for hypertensive retinopathy.
    • Carotids, bruits, JVD, thyromegaly.
    • Cardiac size, murmurs, gallops, arrhythmias, rales.
    • Abdominal masses, bruits, abnormal aortic pulsation.
    • Peripheral pulses, femoral bruits, edema, radial–femoral delay.
    • Neurologic exam and stigmata of gout, dyslipidemia, thyroid dysfunction, Cushing syndrome, neurofibromatosis.

    Baseline tests

    Serum potassium, creatinine, fasting glucose, total and HDL cholesterol, and urinalysis.

    Hypertensive Urgencies, Emergencies & Rapid Reduction

    Definitions

    • Urgent hypertension: SBP ≥240 mmHg or DBP ≥140 mmHg without acute organ-damage symptoms; lower within a few hours.
    • Accelerated hypertension: Retinal hemorrhages or exudates.
    • Malignant hypertension: Papilledema.

    Situations requiring rapid reduction

    • Hypertensive encephalopathy, atherothrombotic brain infarction, subarachnoid or intracerebral hemorrhage.
    • Acute aortic dissection, acute LV failure, acute/impending MI, post-CABG.
    • Acute glomerulonephritis, collagen-vascular renal crises, post-transplant hypertension.
    • Pheochromocytoma crisis or rebound after abrupt withdrawal.
    • Eclampsia.
    • Emergency surgery, postoperative hypertension, suture-line bleeding.
    • Severe burns and severe epistaxis.

    Therapeutic options

    • Oral: Reinstitute prior medications, clonidine, labetalol.
    • Parenteral: Sodium nitroprusside, nitroglycerin, labetalol, nicardipine, enalaprilat.
    Contraindication: Do not use short-acting sublingual nifedipine. It may lower BP unpredictably and cause severe hypotension, tachycardia, myocardial infarction, and coronary steal.
    Treatment

    Goal: Reduce overall cardiovascular morbidity and mortality; target BP <140/90 mmHg.

    Lifestyle

    • Weight reduction.
    • Dietary sodium restriction.
    • Regular exercise.
    • Smoking cessation.
    • Stress reduction / relaxation.
    • Alcohol restriction.

    Initial pharmacologic options

    Low-dose diuretic, β-blocker, ACE inhibitor, ARB, or calcium-channel blocker.

    Diuretics

    • Chlorthalidone or hydrochlorothiazide 12.5 mg daily.
    • Furosemide 40 mg once or twice daily in renal impairment.
    • Spironolactone or potassium supplements if hypokalemia occurs.

    Adrenergic blockers

    • Act centrally, reduce peripheral catecholamine discharge, or block α/β receptors.
    • β-blockers are suitable for young high-renin patients, hyperdynamic circulation, tachycardia/anxiety, previous MI, and alcohol withdrawal.
    • Selective β-blockers are preferred in diabetes; non-selective agents may help migraine or intention tremor.

    Vasodilators and RAAS agents

    • Direct vasodilators: Hydralazine, nitroprusside, nitroglycerin.
    • ACE inhibitors: Excellent first-line choice in diabetes and LV systolic dysfunction; reduce proteinuria and slow diabetic nephropathy. Most common adverse effect: dry irritating cough. Corticosteroids blunt their action.
    • ARBs: More complete angiotensin II blockade; may be initial therapy or combined with ACE inhibitors as stated in the source.
    • Calcium-channel blockers: Nifedipine, amlodipine, diltiazem; effective across races. Verapamil commonly causes constipation. Use caution in systolic dysfunction because of negative inotropy.

    Infective Endocarditis

    Bacteremia + endothelial injury • vegetations • embolic and immune phenomena
    Quick revision: Consider IE in any unexplained fever lasting more than a few days in a predisposed patient. Draw cultures, then start antibiotics immediately; do not wait for late peripheral signs.
    Definition, Organisms & Predisposition

    Infection of heart valves or endocardium, usually bacterial but occasionally rickettsial or fungal. Organisms are often low virulence and disease is typically subacute.

    Responsible factors

    • Bacteremia after minor surgery, dental work/extraction, chewing infected teeth (S. viridans), cardiac surgery, or catheterization (S. aureus).
    • Fungal infection may follow antibiotic cover and reduced resistance after cardiac surgery.
    • A predisposing lesion is usually required, except massive infection in heroin users may involve a normal tricuspid valve.
    • Endothelial damage is caused by turbulent flow.

    Predisposing cardiac lesions

    • Rheumatic disease, especially aortic and mitral insufficiency.
    • Small VSD, PDA, pulmonary stenosis, aortic stenosis, coarctation.
    • Rare in cyanotic disease; does not occur in simple ASD.
    • MVP rarely.
    Pathology & Mechanisms
    • Vegetations form on endocardium exposed to turbulent jets and in low-pressure sink areas.
    • Vegetation center contains bacteria surrounded by fibrin and blood cells; few phagocytes penetrate the fibrin barrier.
    • Uncontrolled infection destroys cusps and may rupture them.
    • Myocardial necrotic foci with cellular infiltration are called Bracht–Wachter bodies.

    Mechanisms of manifestations

    • Toxemia with secondary autoimmune changes.
    • Embolization of detached vegetations.
    • Direct cardiac damage.
    • Increased gamma-globulins may cause false-positive syphilis serology and positive rheumatoid factor.
    • Antigen–antibody–complement complexes cause subendothelial injury, focal nephritis, and diffuse glomerulonephritis.
    Clinical Picture

    Symptoms

    • Continuous, intermittent, or irregular fever, usually low grade.
    • General deterioration, anorexia, weakness.
    • Symptoms from embolic or cardiac complications.

    General signs

    • Marked pallor, toxemia, earthy or café-au-lait skin.
    • Temperature may be only mildly elevated.
    • Tachycardia is constant.
    • Pale clubbing.
    • Osler nodes: Tender purplish intracutaneous nodules on finger/palm pads from endothelial hyperplasia.
    • Splinter hemorrhages: Linear dark subungual streaks from capillary embolization/rupture.
    • Janeway lesions: Painless red-blue dermal millimeter nodules on palms or soles, especially acute bacterial IE.
    • Conjunctival petechiae, sudden blindness, Roth spots.
    • Tender splenomegaly in 80%.
    Organ Involvement & Complications

    Kidney

    • Acute diffuse glomerulonephritis.
    • Focal embolic glomerulonephritis — flea-bitten kidney.
    • Renal infarction → renal angle pain, tenderness, hematuria.

    Nervous system and lungs

    • Embolic hemiplegia, encephalopathy, subarachnoid hemorrhage from ruptured mycotic aneurysm.
    • Recurrent pulmonary infarctions and chest infections.

    Cardiac / overall complications

    • Signs of predisposing lesion; altered old murmur or new murmur.
    • Frequent serious CHF from valvular incompetence or toxic myocarditis.
    • Progressive valvular destruction, severe HF, chronic renal failure, systemic/pulmonary embolization, and treatment complications.
    Investigations
    • Blood cultures: 2–5 cultures over 2–3 successive days; 20–30 cc per culture.
    • ESR: Markedly elevated.
    • Urinalysis: Microscopic or macroscopic hematuria.
    • TTE: Detects vegetations in 80%.
    • TEE: Detects vegetations in 95%.
    • Chest X-ray: Early CHF and multiple small patchy lung infiltrates.

    Clinical rules

    Compliance vs. Elasticity

    Compliance (Expandability): The ability (or distensibility/expansibility) of the lungs or chest wall to expand in response to pressure. High compliance means lungs expand easily (e.g., emphysema). Low compliance means the lung is stiff and resists expansion (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis). Elasticity (Recoil): The tendency of the lungs to return to their original (contracted) shape after being stretched. Lungs are naturally elastic (tend to collapse inward), while the

    chest wall tends to expand outward, creating a dynamic balance. Loss of elasticity increases compliance. Inverse Relationship: A lung with high compliance has low elasticity (expands easily but lacks recoil). A lung with low compliance has high elasticity (resists expansion but recoils strongly).
